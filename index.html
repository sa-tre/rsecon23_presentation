The need for TREs

  • The outputs of research with sensitive data could be signficiant
  • Enabling researchers to work with sensitive data
  • Enforcing and encouraging good security
  • Keeping the confidence of data controllers, subjects and the public
- Working with sensitive data is important/necessary, huge impact - Health data - Administrative data - Commercially sensitive data - All personal data - Research environment need to be productive and secure - Remove the burden from researchers on maintaining security - Particularly when data subjects are the public, we rely on their trust and consent

The current TRE landscape

- Fragmented - Some commercial offerings - Many universities/institutions building/operating their own TREs - Variety of approaches - TREs may be fairly bespoke, focused on a particular use or domain - Some community groups to share expertise, coordinate, unify

A standard architecture

- Enable comparison across TREs - Encourage all TREs to raise standards - Existing standards (ISO27001, CE+) do not make your infrastructure a TRE

Lessons learned

The power of collaboration cafés

  • Collaboration cafés have been the best way to get community input
  • Breakout room discussions have led directly to significant changes
  • Running collaboration cafés requires preparation and planning
  • Facilitators are necessary to make discussions productive
  • Outputs need to be processed
  • Promotion work pays off
- Collaboration cafés have been the best way to get community input - Breakout room discussions have led directly to significant changes - Recognising the important of information governance - Switch from feature focus to capability focus - Modifying the specification - Adjusting project governance and routes to contribution - Changing evaluation process - You need to prepare in advance. A collaboration café should have an agenda and materials prepared - Facilitators keep discussion on topic, help ensure everyone has their say. Especially when many people attend - After a collaboration café the discussion needs to be summarised and translated into actions - We are proud of the number and diversity of people attending. Active promotion and engagement was very worth the investment

The benefits of working in the open

  • People engage better with an open project
  • Start open, stay open
  • Governance aids contributions, but you don't have to be perfect from the outset
  • Contributions can be recognised and incentivise getting involved
- An open project means - People can see and comment on your work in progress - There are clear routes to contribution - You should start open, from the first contribution - Good practice from the beginning - Clear distinction between public and private (e.g. survey) - Making a project open later is difficult - Contribution process, code of conduct _etc._ helps people to contribute - Codifying this takes time. It is OK to be good enough and change your processes when there is a need

Effective engagement takes effort

  • You need to provide scope and context to potential collaborators
  • You must make good use of people's time
  • The "project team" should engage with the collaboration process too
  • Roles like PIE, community managers, RAMs have the skills and enthusiasm to engage people
- Our initial approach was "participant-driven", asking attendees what they wanted to talk about at events - It was difficult for us to keep focus and it was difficult for collaborators to know how they could help - Moved to preparing focused events, asking specific questions - More productive and clear when our approach is not aligned with the community - Time is limited, particularly when engaging with the public - They are not paid to help us, we must respect their time and not waste it - Focus on short and direct engagements with clear goals - Don't expect people to devote time or be engaged over long periods - Your "core team" should be active and visible at collaboration events - It is important to avoid an "us and them" perception - Roles focused on community and engagement have been critical - They bring expertise and a desire to do work RSEs (or other tech focused roles) lack

Get feedback early

  • Initially wasted effort
  • Survey was partially successful
  • Take advantage of version control and open working
  • Changes are more frequent but take less time
- Wasted effort - Trying to produce complete items without feedback - Finding that what we had done was not right - For example the survey - Around two months to create, share, analyse, process results - We are proud, > 100 responses - Strong feedback that survey wasn't particularly helpful, asking wrong questions, wrong focus - Now we take advantage of version control and open working to get feedback earlier - Open discussion about ideas early - Propose changes and ask for feedback - Use collaboration cafés to show what we are working on and improve - More comfortable committing 'draft' items to development branch (releases remain) - Community feedback leads to many, sometimes large changes - Overall it saves effort as we do not invest in the wrong things
